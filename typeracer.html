Introduction

During the summer of 2018 I decided to train myself on "proper" touch typing. I was already familiar with typeracer, and as soon as I found out I could download my data I knew I wanted to make a project out of it.

This scatterplot depicts a high-level summary of my progress thus far. The following sections will explore my typeracer activity, individual races, and WPM improvement (TODO).

Lastly, I would like to credit typeracerdata.com for inspiring or simplifying several parts of this project.

Activity

These charts largely speak for themselves, but there are a few points worth discussing. To preface, the horizontal dotted lines denote the average for that time scale. All dates and times were converted from UTC to local, and they account for daylight savings shifts.

Activity in 2020 was dramatically less than that of adjacent years. One contributing factor may have been the onset of COVID-19 and the turbulence that followed. I fondly look back on the mass exodus from residence during March 2020, and the delightful mess that ensued for the remainder of that semester.

The monthly breakdown was the most surprising to me. I have some ideas to justify each month's activity, but June is quite the mystery. It somehow has a grand total of 3 races across 5 years.

I chose to define marathon as the number of races completed in a single calendar day.

This metric was inspired by typeracerdata.com, although I opted to modify the measure. Typeracerdata.com defines a marathon as the number of races completed in any 24 hour block. This allows your marathon to bridge adjacent calendar days. As such, their metric found my top marathon to be 121 races in 24 hours, starting 2021 November 29, 9:23pm. Each approach is valid and conveys similar but slightly different information.

Measuring marathons by calendar day is easy and simple to grasp, but it fails to incorporate any races after midnight. From the chart above, Race Count by Hour, we can see that I completed a similar number of races between [11:00, 12:00) and [12:00, 1:00), but these adjacent groups are counted separately despite them being contiguous.

Counting any 24-hour block resolves this problem, but a different kind of behaviour arises. Notice my top marathon from typeracerdata.com begins at 9:23pm. This marathon begins with heavy activity at night, zero activity while I slept, and high activity from the afternoon and evening the following day. It is a perfectly valid approach, but it troubles the concept of "marathon" unless you are familiar with how they define it.

Perhaps a better approach, given what we know about this data, would require defining a "marathon day" that lasts 24 hours and begins at 5:00am. This regularly separates race activity (as seen in the Race Count by Hour figure) while maintaining the essence of a marathon.

Common Races

Typeracer allows you to play in several universes, or libraries of texts. To preface, I will be using text, text id, race id, script, and quote interchangably.

Standard universes for many languages exist. Additionally, there are dozens of topical or niche universes such as Dictionary, Jokes, Lord of the Rings, Copypasta, and more. Once again, typeracerdata.com is a tremendous resource for all this information. You can find a list of popular universes here.

I almost exclusive play in the default english universe. Typeracerdata.com suggests there are 7411 unique texts in this universe at the time of writing, and I have completed 2857 unique text ids. Consequently, I have experienced about 38.5% of this universe.

There were 2857 unique text ids among 4567 total races. Roughly speaking, 1/3rd of my races were on quotes I have raced before, and 2/3rds were completely novel.

The bar chart illustrates: among 2857 unique quotes, 1683 of them were raced exactly one time. 785 of them were raced exactly twice, and so on. The most frequently encountered quote was actually a tie - both quotes having been raced 6 times each each.
